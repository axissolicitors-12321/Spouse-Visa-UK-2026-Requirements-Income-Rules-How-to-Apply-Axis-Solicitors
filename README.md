What Is the UK Spouse Visa — and Who Can Apply?
The UK Spouse Visa is a route under the family migration category that allows a foreign national to join their partner and live permanently in the United Kingdom. It is open to:
Married couples where the relationship is legally recognised in the UK
Civil partners
Unmarried couples who have lived together for at least two years
To qualify, the person living in the UK — called the sponsor — must be a British citizen, an Irish citizen, someone with Indefinite Leave to Remain (ILR), a person with EU Settled Status, or a refugee with humanitarian protection in the UK.
Both partners must be at least 18 years old, and you must both genuinely intend to live together in the UK on a permanent basis. The Home Office takes this requirement seriously and will scrutinise your evidence carefully.

The Financial Requirement in 2026: The Biggest Hurdle for Most Families
This is the question we hear most often from clients in Manchester, Birmingham, and London: "Do I earn enough to sponsor my spouse?"
Since April 2024, the minimum income threshold for sponsoring a spouse or partner is £29,000 gross per year. This applies whether you are applying from inside or outside the UK. The previous threshold of £18,600 still applies if your application was submitted before 11 April 2024 and you remain on the same route.
There is no longer an additional income requirement for dependent children, which is a change from the older rules.
You can meet the financial requirement through:
Employment income — supported by 6 months of payslips and bank statements
Self-employment income — supported by tax returns and accountant letters
Pension income of either partner
Cash savings of at least £88,500, held continuously for 6 months
Certain qualifying benefits — in which case the adequate maintenance test applies instead
One important note: if the sponsor receives specific disability-related benefits such as Personal Independence Payment (PIP), Disability Living Allowance, or Carer's Allowance, the income threshold may not apply — but you will still need to demonstrate adequate maintenance and suitable housing.
Many refusals stem not from failing to earn enough, but from submitting incomplete or inconsistent financial documents. A missing payslip, a mislabelled bank statement, or savings not held for the full six-month period can lead to a refusal even when you technically meet the threshold.

Proving Your Relationship Is Genuine: What the Home Office Looks For
The Home Office is not simply checking that two people are married. They are assessing whether the relationship is genuine, ongoing, and not a marriage of convenience.
This means your application needs to tell a convincing story — with evidence that spans your entire relationship, not just the period immediately before you apply.
Strong relationship evidence typically includes:
Your marriage or civil partnership certificate
Photographs together taken over time — not just one trip
Joint bank accounts, utility bills, or tenancy agreements
Regular communication history (messages, call logs, emails)
Travel records showing visits to each other's countries
Statements from family members or friends who know you as a couple
Couples who have spent much of their relationship long-distance face additional scrutiny. The key is not how much time you have spent together physically, but whether you can demonstrate consistent, committed, and credible evidence of a real relationship with clear plans to live together in the UK.

English Language Requirement: What Level Do You Need?
For an initial spouse visa application, you must demonstrate English at CEFR Level A1 — which is a basic conversational level. This can be achieved by:
Passing a UK Visas and Immigration approved Secure English Language Test (SELT)
Holding a degree that was taught and assessed in English
Being a national of a majority English-speaking country
You are exempt if you are over 65 or have a physical or mental condition that prevents you from meeting the requirement.
One common mistake is taking the wrong type of English test — not all tests are accepted by the Home Office, so it is essential to check which tests are UKVI-approved before you book.

How Long Does the Spouse Visa Last — and What Happens Next?
A successful application grants you 2 years and 9 months of leave to remain in the UK (or entry clearance if applying from abroad). After that, you can apply to extend your visa for a further 2 years and 6 months.
After 5 years living in the UK on a spouse visa, you can apply to stay permanently. This is called Indefinite Leave to Remain (ILR), and it gives you the right to live and work in the UK without any visa restrictions. Find out exactly what the Indefinite Leave to Remain (ILR) process involves and whether you are ready to apply.
After ILR, you can apply for British citizenship after a further 12 months, provided you meet the good character and residency requirements.

Extending Your Spouse Visa: What Changes at the Extension Stage
Many couples are surprised to find that the extension is not simply a renewal. The Home Office reassesses your situation afresh, and the requirements shift slightly:
The financial threshold remains at £29,000
The English language requirement increases from CEFR A1 to A2 — a higher level
You must demonstrate that your relationship is still genuine and subsisting
Your accommodation must still be suitable and not overcrowded
If your circumstances have changed since your initial application — a change of employer, a period of reduced income, or a change of address — it is important to address these clearly with supporting evidence rather than hoping the Home Office will overlook them.

The Most Common Reasons Spouse Visa Applications Are Refused
Understanding why applications fail is one of the most valuable things you can do before you apply. Based on the cases we handle at Axis Solicitors, the most frequent reasons for refusal are:
Insufficient relationship evidence. Generic photos, limited messages, and no financial connection between the couple raises immediate questions. The Home Office wants depth, not just documentation.
Incomplete or inconsistent financial documents. Missing payslips, bank statements that do not match payslips, or savings not held for the full six months are among the most avoidable errors.
Wrong English test or expired certificate. Taking a test not approved by the Home Office, or submitting a certificate that has expired, leads to automatic refusal.
Accommodation not evidenced properly. You must show the property is not overcrowded and that landlord permission has been granted if you are renting.
Undisclosed immigration history or criminal records. The Home Office expects full disclosure. Failing to mention a previous refusal or overstay — even from years ago — is treated seriously.
Applying on the wrong visa route. Some applicants apply for a spouse visa when they should be applying for a fiancé(e) visa, or vice versa. The routes have different rules and timelines.
If your application has already been refused, it does not mean your case is over. In most cases there is a right of appeal to the First-tier Tribunal, and our immigration solicitors have a strong track record of overturning Home Office decisions on appeal.

What Has Changed in 2026 — and What Might Change Next
The income threshold increased from £18,600 to £29,000 in April 2025 for new applicants, and proposals to increase it further to £38,700 have not been implemented. No confirmed timetable for further increases has been published.
The May 2025 Immigration White Paper signals potential future modifications to family visa requirements, though specific changes remain subject to consultation and Parliamentary approval.
What this means practically: the rules are more stable in mid-2026 than they were during the upheaval of 2024–2025, but that stability may not last. If you are planning to apply, or if your current visa is approaching its expiry, acting sooner rather than later reduces your exposure to any future rule changes.
